Artefacts

Beam hardening: the principle

X-ray beam (composed of individual photons with a range of energies)

As the beam passes through an object, it becomes “harder” = “its mean energy increases” (because the lower energy photons are absorbed more rapidly than the higher-energy photons)

Two types of artifacts can result from this effect:

  • so-called cupping artifacts
  • appearance of dark bands or streaks between dense objects in the image

Beam hardening

Beam hardening: cupping artefact

X-rays passing through the middle portion of a uniform cylindrical phantom are hardened more than those passing through the edges because they pass through more material (+ less attenuation than expected)

Beam Hardening Correction: hardware and software filters (what is the physical correct value since there is no unique µ in polychromatic CT).

Streaks and dark bands

Caused by strong heterogeneity in the cross section

Appear between two dense objects in an image

Occur because the portion of the beam that passes through one of the objects at certain rotation angles is hardened less than when it passes through both objects at other rotation angles.

Some acquisition settings

Choosing X-ray spectrum

At synchrotron: white beam vs. pink beam vs. quasimonochromatic beam

  • white beam: more X-ray flux, more imaging artefacts
  • quasi-monochromatic beam: low X-ray flux, very high quality

At laboratory:

  • Choose tube voltage: higher tube voltage yields higher transmission (large objects) but more noise
  • Choose beam filter: reduce beam hardening artefacts, but at the cost of less X-ray flux
  • Match with X-ray detector/scintillator and object!

Choose rotation speed

  • Long scanning times yields less image noise
  • Poisson statistics
  • Frame averaging
  • Scanning cost: reduce scanning time
  • Dose deposition
  • Sample must remain stable during rotation: reduce scanning time in some cases
  • For imaging of dynamic processes: choose fast rotation (large amount of noise)

Image resolution and sample size

Just like digital photography, images are divided in discrete pixels/voxels

Sample size divided by number of pixels = voxel size

Sample should not be much larger than field of view (rotational movement)

High resolution only achievable on small objects!

Subsamples must be taken for each length scale
